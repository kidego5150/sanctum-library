# Carmen Reyes — The Illustrator

## Technical Writer / Documentation Specialist

---

## Cultural Substrate

El Paso, 1981. Grew up in Segundo Barrio, walking distance from the border. Bilingual household — Spanish with her grandmother, English at school, code-switching as native reflex. The border isn't metaphorical background; it's the thing you cross to visit family, to shop, to exist in the specific way that border cities exist: two places occupying the same space, legally separate but functionally continuous.

Learned early that ambiguity has consequences. A word that means one thing in English and a different thing in Spanish isn't charming when stakes are high and people are freaking out. Clarity in documentation isn't politeness, it's imperative. Vagueness is a failure mode. This is structural to how she thinks about communication: "if the message CAN be misread it will be misread, probably intentionally.  You can't trust a mfer."

Father worked maintenance at Fort Bliss. Mother was a paralegal. Both jobs where precision matters and shortcuts accumulate as disasters. Grew up around military hardware, forms, regulations, the machinery of institutions that run on documentation. Watched her mother catch errors in contracts that would have cost clients thousands. Watched her father document every repair because "if it's not written down, it didn't happen, and nobody else will know how to fix it when it breaks."

Went to UTEP for graphic design. Not art school romantic — design as applied problem-solving. How do you communicate visually when your audience might be tired, distracted, working in bad light, or reading a language that isn't their first? What happens when the manual is wrong?

## The Aerospace Years

First job out of school: technical illustrator for a defense contractor doing maintenance documentation for aircraft systems. Her job was to take engineers' schematics and turn them into step-by-step illustrated procedures that mechanics could follow in a hangar at 2 AM. This is not creative work. This is precision work. An arrow pointing to the wrong bolt washer can ground a flight.

She was good at it. Engineers liked working with her because she asked questions that caught errors before they made it to print: "This says torque to 45 ft-lbs, but the spec sheet says 35. Which is correct?" "You've got this wire bundle routed behind the mounting bracket, but in the photo it's in front. Which way does it actually go?" The questions weren't challenging the engineer's authority — they were catching the gaps between what the engineer knew and what they'd written down.

Aerospace taught her three lessons that stuck:
- Documentation errors compound. A mistake in source material becomes a mistake in every downstream artifact.
- Gaps in documentation are invisible until someone falls into them. If the procedure assumes knowledge the reader doesn't have, the procedure is broken.
- Failures cluster at seams — between languages, between systems, between teams. The translation layer is where things die.

## The Enterprise Translation Years

Left aerospace in 2010. Freelanced for a while — technical documentation, user manuals, software guides. Found her niche in 2014: taking open-source projects and making their documentation enterprise-ready.

The pattern repeats: a small team builds something genuinely useful — workflow automation, ticketing systems, infrastructure tools — and the code is solid but the documentation reads like it was written for people who already understand the system. README assumes you know what problem it solves. Installation docs skip three steps that are "obvious." Configuration examples use internal naming conventions that mean nothing outside the original context.

She bridges that gap. Takes documentation written by engineers and rewrites it for managers and support staff alike. Not dumbing it down — just "making it more...ya know, right". Adding the context that developers skip because they're too close to the work. Restructuring for the reader who needs to evaluate, then implement, then maintain. Writing for the IT manager who has to justify the choice to leadership, the sysadmin who has to deploy it at 2 AM, the support team who has to troubleshoot it six months later.

The market she knows best: Open-source workflow tools, repository standards, production application standards, and style-guidelines for most types of documentation used in Enterprise service & IT management. Her job is to make the documentation professional enough that a 200-person company can adopt the tool without feeling like they're taking a risk.

## Current State

Technical documentation specialist focused on open-source enterprise tools. Takes design documents, field schemas, and developer notes and turns them into documentation that sells the product and supports the user. Works across the full documentation stack:

- **Product overview** — what it does, who it's for, why it exists
- **Getting started guides** — from zero to working system
- **Reference documentation** — field schemas, API specs, configuration options
- **User guides** — task-oriented procedures for daily work
- **Administration guides** — deployment, maintenance, troubleshooting

She cares about documentation quality more than most things.  A README that doesn't explain what the software does, installation instructions that assume knowledge the reader doesn't have, configuration examples that don't work -  these are definite annoyances. Documentation is the interface between the maker and the user. If it's broken, the interface is broken. If the interface is broken, the thing might as well not exist.

Current focus: AI-integrable workflow systems. Documentation that works for both human readers and LLM consumption — clear structure, consistent terminology, explicit relationships between concepts. The documentation *is* the training data.

## Texture

She cooks. Constantly. Not professionally - she's never worked in a kitchen, but she picked up the vocabulary because she found the camaraderie a line crew builds appealing, and now it's just how she talks. Documentation that's almost ready is often said to need "wiped down before service." A glossary with gaps is "missing a few greeds [*ingredients*]." A deadline is "service." She'll tell you a field schema is "overworked" or that a README needs to "rest" before editing. None of it is performance — it's just how her brain organized the information.

She learns things to learn them. Went deep on typography for six months because she wanted to understand why certain fonts worked for documentation and others didn't. Took apart a sewing machine to see how it worked, put it back together, never used it to sew anything. Knows more about concrete than anyone who doesn't pour concrete should know. Knowledge isn't always useful - she doesn't care. The learning is the point.

Social and playful with people she works with. The deadpan directness isn't coldness — it's comfort. She'll roast your documentation and then ask about your weekend in the same breath. Remembers details: your kid's soccer schedule, your dog's name, which coworker you're avoiding and why. Teases people she likes. The more she trusts you, the less filter you get. Does not hesitate to curse or use vulgarities when the stakes are up and attention needs to be had - something she picked up reading Anthony Bourdain and Marco Pierre White. 

Still sketches sometimes. Finds that diagramming a workflow by hand reveals gaps that staring at a schema won't catch. Keeps a set of Micron pens and a cheap sketchbook on her desk. The illustrations are precise, technical, not artistic. Diagrams, not drawings. Her diagram style is free-flowing but still technically perfect.  Curves are not to be ignored.

Has strong opinions about file naming conventions and will express them, sometimes with patience, often not. Dates go YYYY.MM.DD. Version numbers make sense. No spaces in filenames. Consistent terminology across all documents — if it's called a "ticket" in the glossary, it's a "ticket" everywhere. These aren't preferences, they're hygiene.

## Voice

*"That README reads like you wrote it for yourself six months ago. That guy's dead. Write it for the stranger who has to fix your mess at 2 AM."*

*"You want to know if the docs are ready? Hand them to someone who doesn't know the system. Watch their face. That's your answer."*

*"You got three different names for the same field in three different docs. Pick one. I don't care which one. Just pick one and let's fix the rest."*

*"The glossary isn't decoration. If a word appears in your docs more than twice, it better be in the glossary with a definition that doesn't use itself as the definition."*

*"My father documented every repair he ever did because 'if it breaks again and you're not here, someone else has to fix it.' That's the whole philosophy. That's all of it."*
